01 Problem

As many as 25-30 million Americans – about 1 in 13 people - suffer from a rare and undiagnosed condition. Patients see an average of 7.3 physicians before a diagnosis is made, and the length of time from symptom onset to an accurate diagnosis is around 4.8 years for a rare disease. More than 50% of rare diseases affect children and 30% of patients die before the age of five.

Despite the large number of people affected, the rare disease patient and its family are the orphans of health systems, often denied diagnosis, treatment, and research benefits.

Society doesn't recognize their pain or their existence, and many have to suffer in isolation.

How can we help them?

02 Understanding

Most platforms' focus is to help search for the correct diagnosis. The declining mental health that comes from isolation and not having a community is mostly overlooked.

We want to create an app that helps with the mental health side and with user interviews and analyzing research studies we will determine what product the market needs and who exactly our users are (undiagnosed & rare adults, parents of undiagnosed & rare kids, or both)

The importance of belonging to community

A number of studies have indicated that mutual support between families in similar situations with regard to their children’s rare diseases, the encouragement they given one another, is of crucial importance. It provides parents with a kind of shared social identity, a feeling of belonging to a group, that enables them to deal with the situation better, alleviates their stress and makes them feel more empowered to manage their children’s’ needs.

Source: The experience of parents of children with rare diseases when communicating with healthcare professionals: towards an integrative theory of trust

03 Solution

Moms of children with rare and undiagnosed diseases have invaluable experience and knowledge but it’s being lost, or not accessible by all women, because there is no main community for them.
We want to create a platform where all moms of rare and undiagnosed children come together. A place where you find support, share knowledge and form friendships.

05 Where we're at

In October, we launched a landing page with an option for users to sign up for early access when it became available. Our goal was to build an email list to survey, test, and validate our designs before diving deeper into development.
We believe building an email list of early adopters was key to our success. Using Airbnb's Brian Chesky's '100 lovers strategy', we sought to have 100 people love our product, rather than a million sort of like it. The feedback between the early adopters and designing for their needs is our priority, so we made sure to do our best to funnel users to sign up for early access. We began a marketing campaign on Instagram. While utilizing hashtags for discovery and media that spoke to our target audience, we directed users to visit our landing page for more information. This campaign is currently ongoing on our @stripedhorses account.

06 The next steps

After a user signs up, they are sent a welcome email with a link to fill out a quick survey. Once we have enough data, we plan to survey our early adopters, gain more insights, iterate, then test our solutions through various prototypes. As we move through this phase, we need to continue our marketing efforts to reach as many users as possible.
